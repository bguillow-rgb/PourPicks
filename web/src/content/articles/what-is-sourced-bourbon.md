Whether you've been building a cellar for a decade or just started cataloging your bottles, you'll eventually pick up a bourbon whose brand name doesn't match anything you recognize from a distillery tour. Suddenly you're asking: *did this company actually make this whiskey?* That question leads straight to one of the most misunderstood corners of American whiskey: sourced bourbon and the non-distiller producer, or NDP.

Understanding how sourcing works won't ruin any bourbon you currently love. It will make you a sharper buyer, a more confident cellar curator, and someone who can decode any label in under thirty seconds.

---

## Wait, can a "bourbon brand" sell whiskey it didn't distill?

*A question we hear often:* Yes, and it's far more common than the marketing suggests.

An NDP is any producer that purchases whiskey from another distiller and sells it under their own brand name. The brand designs the label, sets the price, controls the blending or finishing program, and builds the marketing story. The actual distillation happened somewhere else.

This is not a new concept. Rectifiers, those who bought whiskey from producers and modified it, have been around as long as whiskey has existed in America. Some of the biggest brands we know today started as rectifier companies, including Four Roses. The practice went underground during the bourbon boom of the 2010s when new brands were cagey about their origins, but it's been woven into American spirits since before Prohibition.

"Sourced bourbon" simply describes the liquid: the final brand purchases the initial distillate from another company. The brand that sells it is the NDP.

---

## So who actually distills the whiskey that NDPs buy?

*This one comes up a lot:* A handful of large-scale distilleries supply the bulk of NDP liquid in America. An NDP buys whiskey it did not distill, typically from large industrial operations like MGP in Indiana or Bardstown Bourbon Company, then bottles and sells it under its own name.

MGP Ingredients is a significant player, providing whiskey for a wide range of brands. Their distillate quality is high enough that "MGP" has itself become a selling point, Backbone Bourbon, Old Soul, and Smooth Ambler are notable brands that lean into their Hoosier origins rather than hide them.

Sourcing also isn't always all-or-nothing. Some brands distill most of their own whiskey and source the rest. Dickel is a good example: it distills the majority of its lineup but sources its rye from MGP in Lawrenceburg, Indiana. A brand's position on the distiller-to-NDP spectrum can shift release by release.

---

## What exactly does an NDP actually *do* with the whiskey it buys?

*Readers frequently ask:* NDPs are not passive re-labelers, at least the good ones aren't. They buy barrels and add their own touches through some combination of maturation, blending, proofing, and cask finishing. Others work with distilleries to contract-distill new spirit to exacting specifications, customizing everything from grain recipes to the yeast used in fermentation.

Think of an NDP's contribution as happening in layers:

| Layer | What the NDP controls | Flavor impact |
|---|---|---|
| Barrel selection | Choosing individual casks from a distillery's pool | High, single-barrel character varies dramatically |
| Blending | Marrying barrels from one or multiple distilleries | High, creates a house profile independent of the source |
| Cask finishing | Moving whiskey into a secondary barrel (sherry, port, wine) | Medium-high, can reshape the nose and finish entirely |
| Proof at bottling | Adding water, or releasing at barrel strength | Medium, higher proof means more intensity and texture |
| Aging location | Re-racking or additional warehouse time | Medium, climate differences genuinely alter development |
| Transparency | Disclosing or hiding the source distillery | Zero flavor impact, but significant collector/value impact |

A company sourcing unaged distillate has plenty of room to shape the final spirit. Entry proof, barrel char, aging period, aging location, all of it is on the table. NDPs in different parts of the country face different climates, which changes both the rate of aging and the degree of extraction from the wood.

The best NDPs are genuinely creative blending operations. The worst are marketing companies with a label and a broker's phone number.

## How do I spot a sourced bourbon on the label without looking it up online?

This is the single most practical skill in NDP literacy, and the label reveals more than most people realize. The key is knowing which phrases carry legal weight.

"Distilled by" means the company actually made the whiskey. "Produced by," "Bottled by," or "Manufactured by" may mean the bourbon was purchased from another distillery and rebranded, which isn't automatically bad, but it's worth knowing.

If the label says "Bottled by XYZ Spirits, Louisville, KY" but never says "distilled by," XYZ may be buying from someone else. "Produced by" can legally mean the company oversaw production even if another distillery did the actual distilling. "Distilled by" is more specific.

There's also a geographic tell. Large distilleries in Indiana, MGP, now operating as Rossville Union, supplied bulk whiskey to dozens of brands during the 2010s bourbon boom. That's not a quality indicator either way, but it's useful context.

The deepest layer of the label is the DSP number. Every American distillery holds a Distilled Spirits Plant (DSP) number, and the TTB's public COLA database lets you look up any DSP number for free. If the DSP on the bottle points to a facility in a different city or state than the brand's address, you're holding a sourced product.

When you're logging bottles in Pour Picks, the producer field is a good place to capture this distinction. Noting "NDP / sourced from MGP" tells a very different story to your future self than just recording the brand name.

## Is sourced bourbon worth collecting, or should I stick to distillery-direct bottles?

*Readers frequently ask:* The honest answer is that provenance matters differently depending on why you collect.

The most important thing to recognize is that there's nothing necessarily lesser about NDPs. They can be genuinely good whiskeys, equal to or better than distillery releases. Aging, cask selection, and blending all shape the final product. Sourcing is only one part of a much bigger picture.

That said, consumers are getting smarter. People are drinking less overall but asking more questions, not just how old a whiskey is, but how it was matured and why it tastes the way it does. In 2026, that curiosity extends directly to sourcing transparency.

For cellar value and resale, transparency is the key variable. Opaque NDPs, brands that won't disclose the source distillery, carry real collection risk: if the source changes or dries up, so does the consistent profile that made the bottle worth buying. Transparent NDPs with documented barrel programs hold their value more reliably. Limited-edition releases from respected distilleries historically hold value better than novelty bottles or trendy one-offs, and the same logic applies to NDP releases. Documented provenance beats vague storytelling every time.

For drinking, the calculus is simpler. If it tastes extraordinary and the price is fair, the label's production philosophy shouldn't stop you from enjoying it. NDPs can produce genuinely impressive bottles just as readily as full-stack distillers can put out disappointing ones.

## How is an NDP different from an independent bottler?

These two terms get conflated constantly, but they describe distinct business models. NDPs focus on building their own product lines and brand identity. Independent bottlers put the original distillery front and center.

In practice, an independent bottler (IB) typically co-brands its releases with the source distillery, treating cask origin and distillery provenance as the main selling points. Unlike many American NDPs, IBs are generally transparent about where the liquid comes from by design. An NDP, by contrast, builds its own brand as the primary story, the source distillery may appear in fine print or not at all.

The IB model has deep roots in Scotch whisky culture, where independent bottlers like Gordon & MacPhail have operated for over a century. It's an old tradition that remained rare in the United States, where the NDP has been the dominant form of sourced whiskey company. That's starting to shift. A new generation of independent bottlers is focusing on smaller American distilleries, with Vermont's Lost Lantern leading the way in that movement.

## 5+ FAQs

**Is sourced bourbon bad quality?**
Not inherently. Some of the most respected American whiskeys come from NDPs with skilled blenders and rigorous barrel selection. Quality depends on what the NDP adds, expert curation, finishing, or blending, not simply the fact that they didn't distill it.

**What does "distilled by" vs "bottled by" mean on a bourbon label?**
"Distilled by" confirms the named company ran the still. "Bottled by" or "Produced by" means the company purchased and packaged whiskey someone else distilled. If a label never says "distilled by," it's likely sourced.

**Who are the biggest suppliers of sourced bourbon?**
MGP Ingredients in Indiana and Bardstown Bourbon Company in Kentucky are among the most prominent large-scale distilleries supplying bulk whiskey to NDP brands. Heaven Hill and Buffalo Trace have also historically sold surplus barrels to third-party bottlers.

**What is a DSP number and how does it reveal sourcing?**
Every U.S. distillery holds a Distilled Spirits Plant (DSP) number assigned by the TTB. Free online tools let you look up any DSP number and identify the actual distillery of origin. If the DSP on the bottle points to a different facility than the brand's mailing address, you're holding a sourced product.

**Does sourced bourbon affect resale or cellar value?**
Generally yes. Opaque NDPs carry more uncertainty for collectors because a source change can shift the bottle's flavor profile, and its appeal, without warning. Transparent NDPs with consistent, named barrel programs tend to hold secondary value more reliably. The market decides what's collectible, and it usually comes down to bottles that were well-made, well-designed, and fairly priced at release. That principle applies as much to an NDP release as to any distillery-direct bottle.

**Can a great NDP become collectible?**
Absolutely. Barrell Craft Spirits sources casks from established producers and treats blending as the core of what they do. Their commitment to small-batch releases has earned real industry recognition and a dedicated following. Collector demand follows demonstrable quality and consistency, not just distillery provenance.
