A bourbon flight turns a shelf of bottles into a structured lesson. Done well, it sharpens your palate faster than any number of solo pours, surfaces differences you'd never notice glass-by-glass, and gives you honest data on what you actually like -- which makes every future buying decision sharper.

The good news for collectors: your existing cellar is the best raw material you have.

## What exactly is a bourbon flight, and why does it work?

A bourbon flight is a curated set of small pours served together so you can compare different expressions side by side. Most flights contain three to five bourbons, each poured at roughly a half ounce, arranged in a specific order designed to guide you from lightest to boldest.

The mechanism behind why flights accelerate learning is cognitive, not chemical. When you taste one bourbon by itself, your brain shrugs. When you taste several with one thing changing, your brain locks in differences fast -- because it's built to find patterns. That single insight is why a well-built flight teaches you more in an hour than weeks of casual pours.

A flight is not the same as ordering several drinks in sequence. The purpose is comparative: you taste each pour with the others in mind, noticing how the same grain distilled at a different age, proof, or mash bill produces a dramatically different experience in the glass.

## How do I choose a theme for my flight?

*A question we hear often:* collectors with 20+ bottles in their cellar often stall here. The answer is simpler than it looks.

Every strong flight is built around **one changing variable**. Lock everything else down and let that one dimension do the teaching. Here are the most productive themes for collectors building from bottles they already own:

| Theme | What changes | What stays the same | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Distillery deep-dive** | Expression (standard, single barrel, barrel proof) | Distillery, mash bill | Learning a producer's range |
| **Proof progression** | ABV (e.g., 90 -> 100 -> 120+ proof) | Same or similar mash bill | Understanding how proof shapes flavor |
| **Mash bill comparison** | Grain recipe (high-rye vs. wheated vs. four-grain) | Age range, barrel type | Isolating grain character |
| **Age statement ladder** | Years aged (e.g., 4yr -> 8yr -> 12yr) | Same distillery if possible | Hearing what oak does over time |
| **Cask finishing** | Finish type (port, sherry, toasted oak) | Base distillate style | Evaluating whether finishes add or distract |
| **Store pick vs. standard** | Barrel selection | Label, proof, distillery | Judging whether the premium is justified |

The first thing to do when building an at-home bourbon flight is set a theme. You could offer single barrel bourbons or a flight of bourbons everyone should know. Base your bourbon selections on any kind of cohesive theme -- "maybe all wheated bourbons, or all 8 years, or bottles from one distillery" -- that allows you to show off a selection while maintaining an overarching tone.

If you're cataloging your cellar in Pour Picks, filtering by distillery or proof range takes about ten seconds and immediately surfaces natural flight candidates from bottles you already own.

## How many bottles do I need, and how much do I pour?

*This one comes up a lot:* newer collectors sometimes think a flight requires buying bottles specifically for the occasion. It doesn't.

Creating thoughtful bourbon flight ideas elevates your tasting from random sampling to educational exploration. The sweet spot for most tastings is four to six different whiskeys -- enough variety to keep things interesting without overwhelming palates.

For pour size, the collector's standard is **1/2 to 1 oz per expression**. A standard pour is 1-1/2 to 2 ounces for a neat serving, but that doesn't necessarily mean you'll want to offer a full serving from each bottle. Consider how many bourbons you plan on trying. You can pour a little extra if it's only two or three. If you plan on sampling more than six or seven bottles, you might want to tone that back.

For high-proof expressions -- anything above 110 proof -- drop to a half-ounce pour. Choosing the right pour for your at-home tasting doesn't mean avoiding high-proof bourbons; it just means you need to consider that factor. If your tasting revolves around high-proof bourbons, you may go down to a 1/2 oz pour if there are several bottles you want to get through.

At 1/2 oz per pour across five expressions, the math is straightforward: you're tasting roughly 2.5 total ounces -- less than two standard drinks -- which keeps your palate sharp through the entire session.

## What glassware should I use?

Generally, three types of glassware are preferred for a tasting: the Glencairn is considered by many the best glass for tasting and appreciating your whiskey. Its development came through bringing together master blenders from five of the world's largest whiskey companies and perfecting the design.

The right glassware makes a real difference. A Glencairn glass is ideal, thanks to its tulip shape that concentrates aromas while allowing for gentle swirling. If you don't have Glencairns, opt for another quality nosing glass. Use a separate glass for each whiskey to avoid mixing flavors.

For cask-strength or very high-proof expressions, the NEAT glass is worth having. The NEAT glass features a unique rim design that allows alcohol aromas to dissipate, limiting the burn you can sometimes experience from higher-proof bourbons.

One practical note: have water on hand in a plain glass -- not for mixing with the bourbon (do that selectively, intentionally), but for palate cleansing between pours. Encourage yourself to try each pour neat first, then experiment with a few drops of room-temperature water. Water can open up certain flavors and reduce alcohol burn, making subtle notes more accessible -- especially helpful with cask-strength expressions that can be overwhelming at full proof.

## How do I order the pours?

Readers frequently ask: does the sequence matter, or is this just ceremony?

It matters a lot. A bold, high-rye barrel-proof expression early in the flight will desensitize your palate and make everything that follows seem softer and blunter than it is. The reliable rule: **light to bold, low to high proof**.

You may want to arrange your bourbons in a particular order -- perhaps increasing from lowest to highest proof, or sweeter to earthier -- which is highly dependent on the lineup and what you want to highlight in each flight.

If you've got spicy high-rye expressions in your lineup, hold those for later even if the proof isn't particularly high. That rye spice can be intense, and it's better to build up to it than lead with it and numb everyone's tongues for the rest of the session.

For a proof progression flight, a practical ladder looks like: 80-90 proof, then 100-110 proof, then cask strength. For a mash bill flight, lead with the sweeter, softer wheater and finish with the boldest high-rye expression.

## How do I actually run the tasting?

The mechanics are simple. Have everything set up before you start -- glasses poured, notes sheet (or your Pour Picks journal) open, water and palate cleansers ready.

Run the flight like a calm, repeatable routine: nose everything first before you sip (your nose adjusts fast), take some notes, then take at least two sips per pour -- the first sip wakes up your palate, the second sip gives you actual information.

A practical four-step rhythm works well for each expression:

1. **Look** -- color, viscosity, legs on the glass. Deeper amber generally signals more oak contact or a sherry-adjacent finish.
2. **Nose** -- approach the glass slowly, mouth slightly open. Identify broad categories first: sweet or dry? Fruit or grain? Then get specific.
3. **Taste** -- take a small sip and let it coat your entire palate. Don't swallow immediately; instead, let the whiskey rest on your tongue for 3-5 seconds to identify initial flavors. After swallowing, notice the lingering finish.
4. **Compare** -- before moving to the next pour, ask: what's different from the last one? That question is the engine of every good flight.

For palate cleansing between pours: snacks are more than welcome -- and practical. Neutral palate cleansers like water crackers, sliced apples, or unsalted nuts keep taste buds sharp between pours.

If you've got enough glassware and want to really compare, don't finish each pour before moving to the next one. Let yourself go back and forth between glasses and contrast one against the other directly. It creates a more deliberate comparison and helps you understand what makes each bourbon distinct.

## How do I take notes that are actually useful later?

Most collectors either write nothing (relying on memory, which fails) or write purple prose they can't decode six months later. Neither is useful.

The simplest framework that scales: note **four things** per pour -- what you smell before sipping, what hits first on the palate, what develops in the middle, and what the finish does. That's it. Good notes aren't a list of 27 flavors you'll never find again. They're a way to capture direction so you can repeat what you like and avoid what you don't. Use a consistent checklist -- broad categories before specific ones (bright vs. dark, sweet vs. dry, fruit vs. spice). The goal is to write down enough to compare pours honestly, spot patterns, and build your own "this is my kind of bourbon" compass.

After the flight, sort each expression into one of three buckets: expressions you'd pour again without hesitation, ones that surprised you (worth exploring further), and ones that taught you what you don't like. Over time, those patterns tell you more about your palate than any review site can.

## What are some ready-made flight ideas to run tonight?

If you'd rather start tasting than planning, here are five collector-friendly themes you can almost certainly build from bottles already on your shelf -- no purchases required.

- **The Wheater vs. High-Rye Showdown** -- Pull one wheated bourbon (Maker's Mark, W.L. Weller, Larceny) and one high-rye (Four Roses, Bulleit, Knob Creek). Add a third bottle that sits somewhere in the middle. You'll feel the mash bill difference immediately.
- **One Distillery, Three Proofs** -- Buffalo Trace at 90 proof, Eagle Rare at 90 proof, and George T. Stagg or Stagg Jr. at cask strength. Same rickhouse, radically different intensity.
- **The Age Ladder** -- Four Roses Small Batch (no age statement, roughly 5-6 years), Knob Creek 9-Year, and any 12+ year expression you have open. Oak character will step forward with each pour.
- **Store Pick vs. Standard** -- Same brand, same expression, but the standard release against a store-selected single barrel. If the pick is worth the premium, this is where you find out.
- **The Cask Finish Flight** -- Standard bourbon, then two finished expressions (port, sherry, or toasted oak). Useful for deciding whether you're a finishing advocate or a purist.

The pivot toward craftsmanship, barrel-specific selection, and flavor-driven releases continues to shape the bourbon world. Small-batch, single-barrel, and craft distiller bourbons are gaining in popularity as collectors and drinkers prioritize taste and uniqueness over rarity. Flights are how you keep pace with that shift -- by systematically understanding what you're drinking rather than just accumulating bottles.
