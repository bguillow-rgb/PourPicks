What Is a Bourbon Vertical Tasting? A Collector's Guide

A bourbon vertical tasting compares multiple releases from one distillery side-by-side to reveal how age, proof, and barrel variation shape flavor. Here's how to run one.

By Pour Picks · Published July 5, 2026

In brief

A bourbon vertical tasting is a side-by-side comparison of multiple releases from the same distillery -- typically different age statements, annual batches, or expressions -- to isolate how time, proof, and barrel variation shape flavor. It's the single most educational tasting format for collectors and requires as few as three bottles.

A vertical tasting is one of the most rewarding things you can do with a bourbon collection — and one of the most underexplained. If you’ve been cataloging your cellar and wondering what to actually do with multiple releases from the same distillery, this is your answer.

What exactly is a bourbon vertical tasting?

At its simplest, a vertical tasting puts several releases from the same distillery side-by-side so you can taste how their character evolves across age, proof, or production year. “To me, a vertical is tasting the different expressions produced by the same distillery,” in the words of experienced tasting room operators — you’re looking to identify similarities and differences within those expressions. The goal isn’t to declare a winner; it’s to understand what a single distillery’s DNA looks and tastes like when you strip away everything except time and wood.

Contrast that with a horizontal tasting: a vertical tasting covers products from the same distillery — several kinds of Four Roses, for instance — while a horizontal tasting covers the same type of bourbon or whiskey from different distilleries, like three different ryes. Verticals teach you one house’s character in depth. Horizontals teach you how the category varies at large. Both are worth doing, and most collectors find verticals more immediately actionable when building a focused cellar.

Okay, but what’s the point for someone who already collects?

A question we hear often: if you’re already tracking your bottles and writing tasting notes, why bother organizing a formal vertical?

Because tasting in isolation and tasting comparatively are completely different experiences. When you nose a 10-year expression next to the 15-year from the same mash bill, your brain has a reference point it didn’t have before. You stop describing flavors in the abstract and start describing change — the way oak integration deepens, how a fruit note softens or disappears, why the higher-proof release feels simultaneously bolder and more complex on the finish.

For collectors specifically, verticals have a second payoff: they sharpen your ability to evaluate future releases before you buy. If you’ve done a thorough Four Roses vertical and have a precise sense of what their low-rye mash bill tastes like at 10, 12, and 15 years, you have a real benchmark when a new limited release hits shelves. You’re not guessing — you’re extrapolating from firsthand data. That kind of sensory library is genuinely hard to build any other way. Pour Picks’ tasting journal is a natural home for those comparative notes; tagging entries by distillery makes it easy to pull up your full vertical history before a purchase decision.

How is a vertical different from just opening a bunch of bottles?

This one comes up a lot: the structure matters more than most people expect.

Whatever expressions you choose, it’s best to start with the lowest-proof bourbon and work your way up to the highest proof, so that your tastebuds don’t get overwhelmed immediately. Beyond proof order, think about pour size — one ounce per expression is the standard, enough to evaluate nose, palate, and finish without rushing. How many expressions do you need? The sweet spot that experienced tasters land on is five or six bottles — enough to explore variety without going overboard.

Have still water and something plain and starchy (unsalted crackers work well) between pours. Opening all bottles about half an hour beforehand lets them breathe, and glasses of water in position to rinse palates between each pour makes a real difference. And resist the urge to check tasting notes before you’ve written your own. Pre-reading colors your perception in ways that make the exercise less valuable.

What distilleries make the best starting verticals?

Readers frequently ask: where do I even begin if I haven’t done this before?

The best verticals come from distilleries with clearly differentiated expressions built on a consistent mash bill. If the expressions are too similar, you spend the evening confirming that they taste alike. If they’re too different — different mash bills, wildly different proof — you’re running a horizontal in disguise.

To truly appreciate what a single distillery is doing, look toward one with a less sprawling portfolio that offers clear variation between expressions. Old Forester’s Whiskey Row Series or Bardstown Bourbon Company’s Origin Series are strong examples. For collectors who already have access to annual releases, a vertical tasting done by picking different whiskeys from the same distillery from different years is the purest format — particularly effective with single-barrel bottlings, where each release carries a specific barrel number and dump date.

Single barrel whiskeys tell you the year and often the batch number, which gives you a precise foothold to work with when comparing across a vertical. That’s a meaningful edge over blended annual releases, where year-to-year differences can be subtle by design. “Year to year your distillate should be consistent before it goes into the barrel,” as one master distiller explains — but “we’re working with Mother Nature, so you’re always going to get little different nuances.” Those nuances are exactly what a well-structured vertical is designed to surface.

A few starting lineups worth considering:

  • Elijah Craig — standard small batch, barrel proof (any batch), and 18-year; three very distinct points on one mash bill
  • Four Roses — OBSK, OESO, and Small Batch Select reveals how yeast and grain interact
  • Old Forester Whiskey Row — 1870, 1897, 1910, and 1920 offer proof and process variation in a clean sequence
  • Pinhook Vertical Series — purpose-built for the format; the concept behind the series is to minimize year-to-year variables by using an identical mash bill, resulting in nearly identical products with just one primary difference in how the proof is adjusted to best complement the resultant whiskey.

Does adding a drop of water change anything?

Yes — and intentionally so. One of the best ways to open the flavor of bourbon and help develop your tasting skills is to incorporate water. “A drop or two of water should be added after an initial taste neat, as the chemical reaction will boost the concentrations of flavor compounds and ‘open’ or ‘release’ aromatics to further explore the depth of the whiskey.”

In a vertical, this becomes especially revealing. The younger or lower-proof expression may open dramatically with water while the higher-proof aged release barely changes — or vice versa. Tasting each expression neat first, then with a few drops of water, effectively doubles your data points and often surfaces a second layer of comparisons you’d have missed entirely.

How do I document a vertical so I actually remember what I tasted?

Readers frequently ask: tasting notes made during a vertical feel different from solo session notes — there’s more context, more contrast, and more to capture.

The most useful vertical notes record not just what each expression smells and tastes like in isolation, but how it differs from the others in the flight. Write a brief comparison after each pour: “fruitier and less oaky than the 10-year; the rye spice that was subtle there becomes the dominant note here.” Those relational observations are what a vertical is uniquely positioned to generate, and they’re far more useful than standalone descriptors when you’re trying to decide whether to acquire the next release in the series.

Keep pour size consistent — one ounce is the standard — and log the order you tasted so you can account for palate fatigue when you review your notes later. A vertical tasting samples multiple ages of the same brand to isolate the effect of barrel aging; a horizontal samples different mash bills at similar ages and proofs to reveal how grain changes spice, sweetness, and mouthfeel. Your notes should reflect which variable you were actually testing. Logging verticals inside Pour Picks’ journal and linking each entry to the bottle in your cellar keeps the full picture connected — you can see your tasting history alongside each bottle’s acquisition date, purchase price, and current estimated value.

What’s happening in the collector market that makes verticals more relevant now?

The bourbon resale market isn’t collapsing — it’s maturing. Collectors are becoming more selective about what deserves a premium. That shift changes how serious collectors think about building a cellar. Instead of chasing unicorns, many enthusiasts have shifted toward “worth it” bottles: bourbons that balance flavor, maturity, and value over hype — bottles that deliver serious quality at a justifiable price rather than chasing scarcity alone.

In that context, the vertical format fits the current moment well. The clearest market theme right now is bifurcation: ultra-aged and cask strength expressions have dominated gains, with several aged expressions surging significantly, reflecting renewed collector appetite for rare, high-proof aged releases with demonstrable scarcity. Collectors who have done thorough verticals within a distillery’s lineup are better positioned to identify which specific expressions in a series represent genuine quality step-ups — and which are just priced that way.

The secondary market data reinforces this. The secondary market has moved away from social media “hype” toward data-driven auction results. Collectors who combine hands-on vertical tasting with real pricing data — the kind available through cellar tracking tools — are making buying and holding decisions on evidence rather than reputation.

A vertical tasting doesn’t require a special occasion or a rare collection. Three bottles from the same distillery, poured in proof order, with a notebook nearby, is enough to start building the kind of firsthand sensory knowledge that no review can give you. The collection you’ve already built is the starting point.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a vertical and a horizontal bourbon tasting?

A vertical tasting stays within one distillery and compares different expressions, ages, or annual releases. A horizontal tasting compares the same style of bourbon -- say, wheated bourbons or high-rye expressions -- across multiple distilleries. Verticals teach you a distillery's house character; horizontals teach you how grain and process vary across the category.

How many bottles do you need for a bourbon vertical?

Three bottles is a workable minimum that lets you spot a clear progression. Five or six is often considered the sweet spot -- enough variety to reveal meaningful patterns without overwhelming palates. Going beyond seven bottles in one session tends to produce diminishing returns as sensory fatigue sets in.

Should you taste blind or labeled in a vertical?

Both work, and the choice shapes what you learn. Blind tasting removes label bias and forces honest sensory evaluation -- you'll discover whether you actually prefer the 10-year or the 15-year without the price tag influencing you. Labeled tasting is better for educational sessions where provenance, release year, and production notes are part of the conversation.

What order should you taste bourbons in a vertical?

Start with the lowest proof and youngest expression, then work upward in proof and/or age. This prevents high-ABV, heavily oaked pours from desensitizing your palate before you reach the subtler bottles. Palate cleansers like plain water or unsalted crackers between pours help reset between expressions.

What's the best distillery lineup for a first bourbon vertical?

Distilleries with clearly distinct expressions across a consistent mash bill make the best starting points. Four Roses (multiple recipes and age statements), Old Forester's Whiskey Row Series, and Elijah Craig's standard/barrel proof/18-year lineup are all well-regarded beginner verticals because the differences between expressions are pronounced and the bottles are relatively accessible.

Can you run a vertical tasting with annual releases of the same product?

Yes -- and many collectors consider this the purest form of vertical. Comparing, say, the 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024 releases of the same bottled-in-bond expression isolates variables like weather, barrel placement, and distillery process refinements. Single-barrel releases are especially informative because each bottle carries a dump date and barrel number, giving you a precise timeline to work with.
