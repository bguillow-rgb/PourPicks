What Is Sour Mash Bourbon? A Collector's Guide

By Pour Picks · Published July 21, 2026

Quick Answer

Sour mash is a fermentation technique, not a flavor descriptor, in which a portion of acidic liquid left over from a previous distillation run (called backset) is added to each new batch of fermenting grain. The process stabilizes pH, controls unwanted bacteria, and promotes batch-to-batch consistency. Nearly all major Kentucky bourbons use it.

If you’ve ever picked up a bottle of Jim Beam, Maker’s Mark, or Wild Turkey and noticed the words “sour mash” on the label, you’re not alone in wondering what it actually means. It sounds like it might describe the taste, something acidic or sharp. It doesn’t. Sour mash is one of the most widely used but least understood terms in American whiskey, and for a collector who cares about what’s in the bottle, understanding it changes how you read a label entirely.

”Wait - does ‘sour mash’ mean my bourbon is going to taste sour?”

A question we hear often:

No. And this misconception trips up even experienced drinkers.

The name is genuinely misleading: sour mash bourbon does not taste sour. The word “sour” refers to the acidic nature of the backset, not to any flavor characteristic in the finished whiskey. Pour yourself a glass and you’ll find the usual bourbon signatures, caramel, vanilla, baking spice, oak. Nothing resembling vinegar or sourdough.

Sour mash is a fermentation method, not a description of how the whiskey tastes. The confusion is understandable because the word is printed prominently on some labels and most bottles don’t explain what it means. Think of it less like a flavor term and more like a production note, a clue about how the whiskey was made, not what it will taste like in your glass.

So what exactly is sour mash, and how does it work?

Sour mash bourbon is produced using a fermentation technique in which a portion of the acidic liquid left over from a previous distillation run is added to each new batch of fermenting grain mash. That leftover liquid is called backset, though you’ll also see it referred to as stillage, setback, or spent beer.

The most useful way to picture it: bakers use part of an existing sourdough starter to guide the next loaf. Distillers do something similar, using backset to create a stable, predictable environment for yeast.

When a batch of bourbon runs through the still, two things come out: the alcohol-rich vapor that becomes whiskey, and the spent, non-alcoholic liquid left behind. That leftover liquid is the backset, and it’s naturally acidic. Adding it to the next fermenter helps maintain consistency from batch to batch and cuts down on bacterial contamination during fermentation.

The ratio matters in practice. Typically distilleries use a 1-to-3 or 1-to-4 ratio, meaning roughly 25%-33% of each new fermenter volume is backset from the previous run.

Why did distillers start doing this in the first place?

This one comes up a lot:

Bourbon’s early production environment was hostile. Fresh grain mash has a relatively neutral pH, which is an ideal breeding ground for bacteria. Distillers discovered that recycling acidic backset into a new batch dropped the fermenter’s pH, crowding out competing microorganisms and giving yeast a cleaner run at the work.

Consistency is the other big factor. A distillery producing whiskey year-round wants each batch to behave like the last one. Backset carries mineral compounds, yeast byproducts, and chemical signatures from the previous fermentation, so the new batch inherits something from the one before it, which inherited something from the one before that. This chemical continuity is a large part of why a bottle of Jim Beam White Label from 2025 tastes essentially the same as one from 2005, even though no single ingredient was literally carried forward.

There’s also a subtler flavor benefit. Backset helps with esterification. Esters, which provide the fruity top notes in whiskey, form through the combination of alcohol and acid, and certain esterification only occurs at specific pH levels. Controlling pH isn’t just about keeping the bad stuff out; it also coaxes specific flavors in.

The process has a well-documented history. The first known recipe for sour mash was recorded in 1818 by Catherine Carpenter of Casey County, Kentucky. According to the Kentucky Distillers’ Association, Carpenter’s written record predates the more famous name associated with the process and represents the first known formalization of the technique. It has been the dominant fermentation method in Kentucky bourbon production for nearly two centuries.

How is sour mash different from sweet mash?

FeatureSour MashSweet Mash
Fermentation baseBackset from prior batch + fresh water + grainFresh water + grain only
pH controlNaturally acidic; tightly regulatedLess controlled; more variable
Batch consistencyHigh, chemical thread links each runLower, each batch starts fresh
Bacterial riskReduced by acidic environmentHigher; requires more diligence
Flavor characterSofter, creamier mouthfeel with mellow, well-integrated flavorsBrighter, more expressive per batch
Industry prevalenceDominant, used by most major distilleriesNiche; growing craft interest
Notable producersJim Beam, Wild Turkey, Maker’s Mark, Woodford ReservePeerless, Wilderness Trail

Without the built-in acidity of backset, fermentation pH is less controlled, and fermentation can get unpredictable. Some distillers like sweet mash precisely because each batch stands on its own and can show real variation. Others prefer sour mash for the stability and fewer surprises. Neither is wrong; they’re just different bets.

Which bourbons actually use sour mash?

Readers frequently ask:

Almost all of them. The practice has been the dominant fermentation method in Kentucky bourbon production for nearly two centuries and is used by the vast majority of major distilleries today.

Jim Beam, Jack Daniel’s, Wild Turkey, Maker’s Mark, and Woodford Reserve are five of many bourbon and Tennessee whiskey producers where sour mash is the standard. Jack Daniel’s even prints it on the label: “Tennessee Sour Mash Whiskey.” With every new batch, the distillery adds backset from the previous fermentation, keeping the environment stable, helping prevent contamination, and supporting the consistent flavor the brand is known for.

What’s worth noting for collectors is that most bottles don’t print “sour mash” prominently, not because they skip the process, but because it became so universal that it stopped feeling like a differentiator. When you do see it on a label, it’s as much a marketing choice as an informational one.

The exceptions are craft producers experimenting with sweet mash: distilleries like Wilderness Trail and Kentucky Peerless, who use fresh-mash fermentation specifically to let each batch express its own character. They’re accepting more variability in exchange for potentially higher expressiveness.

Does the sour mash process affect what’s in my cellar, practically speaking?

For most collectors, sour mash matters in one specific way: consistency. Using backset from previous batches is how distilleries keep their flavors stable across years of production. That’s genuinely useful when you’re building a vertical or comparing a current release to an older bottle. The fermentation backbone of a Jim Beam or a Wild Turkey should hold steady across years, even as other variables like aging location and warehouse position shift things around.

It also sets expectations when you crack a sweet mash expression. Because those batches start from scratch, there’s more natural batch-to-batch variance, which can produce exciting outliers, but also means the bottle you tasted last year may differ noticeably from this year’s release. If you’re logging tasting notes in Pour Picks, that’s worth tracking: noting “sweet mash” alongside your flavor observations gives you production context that explains why two consecutive releases from the same label can taste like siblings rather than twins.

Is sour mash required by law for bourbon?

No. Most bourbon distillers use the sour mash method, but it is an option, not a legal requirement for bourbon or Tennessee whiskey. The TTB’s legal definition of bourbon covers grain bill, barrel type, distillation proof, and entry proof, with nothing about fermentation method. A distillery can produce a fully legal, label-compliant straight bourbon using nothing but fresh water and fresh grain in every batch.

This is worth knowing when you encounter a “sweet mash” bourbon at a craft distillery tasting room. It isn’t a shortcut or a deviation from tradition. It’s a deliberate production choice that predates the sour mash era and is seeing a genuine craft revival.

FAQs

Does sour mash bourbon taste sour? No. The word “sour” refers to the acidic pH of the backset, not to any flavor in the finished whiskey. A sour mash bourbon tastes like bourbon, sweet, oaky, and spiced, not vinegary or tart.

Is sour mash a legal requirement for bourbon? No. Sour mash is an industry-standard practice but is not legally required to call a spirit bourbon. A bourbon can be made using the sweet mash method and still meet all TTB legal definitions.

What is backset in bourbon production? Backset (also called stillage, setback, or spent beer) is the thin, acidic, non-alcoholic liquid that drains from the still after a batch of fermented mash has been distilled. Distillers add it to the next batch’s fermenter to lower pH and stabilize fermentation.

What is the difference between sour mash and sweet mash bourbon? Sour mash bourbon uses backset from a previous batch to acidify and stabilize fermentation, producing consistent batch-to-batch flavor. Sweet mash uses only fresh water and grain, giving each batch an independent character with potentially more variation, but also less predictability.

Which popular bourbons use the sour mash process? The vast majority of major Kentucky and Tennessee whiskeys use sour mash, including Jim Beam, Wild Turkey, Woodford Reserve, Maker’s Mark, Evan Williams, and Jack Daniel’s (labeled “Tennessee Sour Mash Whiskey”). Notable sweet mash producers include Peerless and Wilderness Trail.
