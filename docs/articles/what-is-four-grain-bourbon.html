What Is a Four Grain Bourbon? A Collector's Guide

Four grain bourbon uses corn, rye, wheat, AND malted barley for flavor — not just the standard three. Here's what it tastes like and why collectors seek it out.

By Pour Picks · Published July 19, 2026

In brief

Four grain bourbon uses all four grains, corn, rye, wheat, and malted barley, as intentional flavor contributors. Most bourbons use only corn plus one secondary grain. Four grain expressions layer rye's spice and wheat's softness simultaneously, producing a more complex, multi-stage palate that collectors prize for its uniqueness.

Four grain bourbon sits at an interesting intersection of craft ambition and mash bill science. Most bottles in your cellar are built around three grains: corn as the legal majority, one secondary flavor grain (rye or wheat), and a small functional portion of malted barley. Four grain breaks from that convention by treating all four grains as intentional flavor contributors rather than assigning barley a purely behind-the-scenes role. The result is a style that collectors increasingly seek out precisely because it refuses to live in the established wheated or high-rye camps.

What exactly makes a bourbon “four grain,” and why does it matter?

Bourbon is made primarily from corn, at least 51% of the grain mixture by federal law. The remaining grains, most commonly rye, wheat, and malted barley, are called flavor grains, and the specific combination a distillery chooses is its mash bill.

In standard production, a distillery picks a lane. Most bourbons fall into one of two broad style categories defined entirely by the secondary grain choice. Rye-forward bourbons use rye at anywhere from 10% to 35% of the mash bill, producing the classic Kentucky profile of sweet corn character undercut by dry, peppery spice. Wheated bourbons swap rye for wheat and move in a softer, rounder direction entirely.

Four grain refuses that either/or decision. It blends wheat and rye into the same recipe alongside corn and malted barley. Rather than committing to one flavor path, it tries to balance softness and spice, and when well executed, the result feels genuinely layered. The idea is to capture something from each grain: sweetness from corn, spice from rye, softness from wheat, and the enzymatic contribution plus subtle notes from malted barley.

That last point about malted barley is where four grain gets interesting for collectors. Malted barley is present in virtually every bourbon mash bill regardless of style, typically at 5% to 15%. Its primary role is enzymatic: malting the barley produces the amylase enzymes that convert the other grains’ starches into fermentable sugars. In a four grain expression, the distiller intentionally bumps the barley percentage, or at minimum lets all four grains pull flavor weight together, rather than treating barley as a silent workhorse.

How is four grain different from wheated bourbon or high-rye bourbon?

A question we hear often: collectors who already know the wheated-vs-high-rye axis sometimes assume four grain is just a blended compromise. It isn’t. It’s a structurally different recipe category.

In a four grain, wheat supplies pastry-like sweetness and a round mouthfeel. Rye contributes pepper, clove, and herbal lift. Barley offers subtle nutty notes and helps fermentation along. The glass moves across the palate in stages: a sip might open with caramel and honey, shift to cinnamon and orange peel, then settle into a malty finish.

That movement, the way a pour develops rather than hitting one consistent note, is why serious collectors find four grain worth dedicating shelf space to. It rewards slower, more attentive drinking, which maps well to the journaling and tasting-note workflow that many cellar-builders use.

Which four grain bourbons should I actually look for?

This one comes up a lot: knowing the concept is one thing; knowing the specific bottles worth hunting is another.

E.H. Taylor, Jr. Four Grain (a highly sought-after limited release from Buffalo Trace), Laws Four Grain Bourbon (Colorado), Few Bourbon (Illinois), and Penelope Four Grain are among the most widely cited in the category. Major Kentucky distilleries seldom go this route, but a number of craft producers have built four grain releases into their core programs.

E.H. Taylor Four Grain is probably the collector’s benchmark here: a limited annual release that routinely draws significant secondary market attention. Laws Whiskey House in Denver represents the craft side of the ledger, sourcing all grain locally from Colorado farms. Penelope and Old Elk have both found audiences among buyers who want something outside the standard rye-or-wheat binary.

The broader trend toward heritage and specialty grains has accelerated four grain’s profile through 2025 and into 2026. Some distillers are pushing further by incorporating specialty grains like oats, which can add creaminess and a nutty, granola-like quality. Buffalo Trace’s experimental Daniel Weller line has explored ancient grain variants like spelt and emmer wheat, pointing toward where the specialty-grain category may head next.

Does the four grain mash bill actually translate to noticeably different flavor, or is it marketing?

Readers frequently ask: this is a fair skeptical question worth answering honestly.

The science supports a real difference, not just a story on the label. Grain ratios establish the flavor precursors that carry through fermentation, distillation, and aging, and the mash bill is one of the most consequential decisions in production because no subsequent step can fundamentally undo it. Two bourbons from the same distillery, aged in identical barrels for the same length of time, can taste noticeably different simply because one uses rye and the other uses wheat as the secondary grain. A four grain recipe compounds this: both rye and wheat are present, so both sets of flavor precursors are in the distillate from day one.

That said, proportion matters enormously. A four grain recipe with 2% each of rye and wheat won’t behave the same way as one with 15% of each. When evaluating four grain bottles, look for distilleries that publish actual percentages. Transparency about grain bills is increasingly a mark of quality producers, and according to whiskey industry observers, consumer demand for that kind of transparency has risen sharply through 2025 and into 2026, with buyers specifically asking about mash bills, barrel programs, and grain sourcing rather than just age and proof.

How does four grain bourbon age compared to other styles?

Four grain aging sits in genuinely interesting territory. Some argue that wheat ages particularly gracefully and moves further forward in the flavor profile the longer it rests in the barrel, while rye tends to compete with wood for palate share as it ages. A four grain expression has both dynamics happening simultaneously, which means longer maturation can shift the flavor balance in ways that are harder to predict than in single-secondary-grain bourbons. That makes older four grain releases particularly compelling for vertical tastings. The question of which grain “wins” at 8 years vs. 12 years is genuinely answered differently bottle to bottle.

The wheat influence in a four grain can act as a cushion against over-oaking in older expressions. The rye component, meanwhile, keeps the profile from going purely soft and one-dimensional. For collectors who cellar for the long game, a well-made four grain at 10+ years can be one of the most structurally interesting pours in the rack.

How should I log four grain bottles in my collection?

When cataloging four grain releases, the mash bill detail is worth capturing beyond just the brand name. A good cellar record for a four grain bottle should note the grain percentages (if published), whether it’s a limited or annual release, its proof at bottling, and your tasting notes specifically tracking whether the rye or wheat character dominates, since that balance shifts across releases and vintages.

FAQs

Is four grain bourbon the same as wheated bourbon? No. Wheated bourbon replaces rye with wheat, using three grains total. Four grain bourbon keeps both rye and wheat alongside corn and malted barley, balancing spice and softness in the same bottle.

Does malted barley count as a flavor grain in four grain bourbon? Malted barley’s primary role in any bourbon is enzymatic: it converts starches to fermentable sugars. But in elevated percentages above the standard 5%-10%, it contributes subtle nutty, biscuity notes that become a genuine flavor component, which is the intent in four grain recipes.

What are some well-known four grain bourbon bottles to try? E.H. Taylor Four Grain (Buffalo Trace), Laws Four Grain Straight Bourbon (Colorado), Penelope Four Grain, Old Elk Four Grain, and Few Bourbon (Illinois) are among the most widely cited examples in the collector community.

Why is four grain bourbon less common from large Kentucky distilleries? Major Kentucky distilleries built their reputations and aging programs around specific two- or three-grain mash bills optimized over decades. Using both rye and wheat adds production complexity and can blur a distillery’s house style, so most stick with one secondary flavor grain.

How should I store four grain bourbon in my cellar? Exactly like any other bourbon: upright, away from direct light and heat, with the cork or cap sealed tightly. The four grain mash bill doesn’t change storage requirements, and the same oxidation risks apply once the bottle is opened.
